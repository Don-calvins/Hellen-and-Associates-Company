Introduction

When it comes to fixed-income investments in Kenya, Treasury Bills (T-bills) and Corporate Bonds are two of the most popular options. Both offer regular income and are considered lower risk than stocks, but they have distinct characteristics that make them suitable for different types of investors.

In this comprehensive guide, we'll compare Treasury Bills and Corporate Bonds across every important dimension: returns, risk, liquidity, minimum investment, and tax treatment. By the end, you'll know exactly which option fits your investment goals.

📌 Market Update 2024

Current T-bill rates range from 10-14% while top corporate bonds offer 12-16%. With interest rates at historic highs, now is an excellent time to consider fixed-income investments.

What are Treasury Bills?

🏛️

Treasury Bills (T-Bills)

Short-term government securities issued by the Central Bank of Kenya on behalf of the government.

  • Issuer: Government of Kenya (CBK)
  • Tenure: 91 days, 182 days, 364 days
  • Minimum Investment: KES 50,000
  • Risk Level: Virtually risk-free (government guaranteed)
  • Returns: 10-14% (as of 2024)
  • Liquidity: Can be sold in secondary market

    What are Corporate Bonds?

    🏢

    Corporate Bonds

    Debt securities issued by companies to raise capital, promising to pay interest periodically and return principal at maturity.

  • Issuer: Private companies (e.g., Safaricom, Kengen, East African Breweries)
  • Tenure: 2-10 years (typically)
  • Minimum Investment: KES 50,000 - KES 100,000
  • Risk Level: Low to medium (depends on company credit rating)
  • Returns: 12-16% (as of 2024)
  • Liquidity: Traded on Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE)

    Interest Rate Risk

    How to Invest

    Treasury Bills

    Step 1
    Open a CDS account with CBK or an authorized agent (KES 1,000 fee)
    Step 2
    Fund your account with minimum KES 50,000
    Step 3
    Apply through "Dhow" platform or visit CBK branch
    Step 4
    Participate in weekly auctions (Tuesdays for 91/182-day, Thursdays for 364-day)

    Corporate Bonds

    Step 1
    Open a CDS account with a stockbroker or investment bank
    Step 2
    Apply during bond offer period (typically 2-3 weeks)
    Step 3
    Pay for bonds through your CDS account
    Step 4
    Trade on NSE secondary market after listing

    💡 Pro Tip

    You can also invest in both T-bills and bonds through money market funds, which offer lower minimums (KES 100) and professional management.

    Understanding Yields

    T-bills are sold at a discount. For example:

    • Face Value: KES 100,000
    • Purchase Price: KES 88,000
    • Discount: KES 12,000
    • Yield: 13.6% (12,000 / 88,000)

    Corporate bonds pay regular interest (coupons):

    • Face Value: KES 100,000
    • Coupon Rate: 14% per year
    • Annual Interest: KES 14,000 (paid semi-annually)

    Tax Implications

    Treasury Bills

    • 15% withholding tax on interest
    • Deducted at source
    • No additional filing required
    • Tax-exempt for pension funds

    Corporate Bonds

    • 15% withholding tax on interest
    • Deducted at source
    • Capital gains tax on sale (if held < 3 years)
    • 5% capital gains tax on disposal

    ⚠️ Important

    Capital gains tax of 5% applies to profits from selling bonds within 3 years of purchase. After 3 years, no capital gains tax.

    Liquidity Comparison

    T-Bill Liquidity

    • Can be sold before maturity through CBK
    • Limited secondary market
    • Best to hold to maturity
    • Can be used as collateral for loans

    Corporate Bond Liquidity

    • Actively traded on NSE
    • Can sell anytime during market hours
    • Prices quoted daily
    • Easy to exit before maturity

    Pros and Cons

    ✅ T-Bill Pros

    • Government guaranteed - virtually risk-free
    • Short tenure - quick access to funds
    • Weekly auctions - always available
    • Simple to understand
    • No capital gains tax

    ❌ T-Bill Cons

    • Lower returns than bonds
    • Less liquid secondary market
    • Discounted interest only at maturity
    • Inflation can erode real returns

    ✅ Corporate Bond Pros

    • Higher returns than T-bills
    • Regular interest income (coupons)
    • Active secondary market on NSE
    • Capital appreciation potential
    • Diversification benefits

    ❌ Corporate Bond Cons

    • Higher risk than T-bills
    • Longer tenure - interest rate risk
    • Capital gains tax if sold early
    • Company default risk
    • Less frequent new issues

    Which Should You Choose?

    Choose Treasury Bills if:

    • You need maximum safety for your money
    • You're investing for less than 1 year
    • You want simple, predictable returns
    • You're risk-averse or nearing retirement
    • You need liquidity but not trading

    Choose Corporate Bonds if:

    • You can accept slightly higher risk for better returns
    • You want regular income (quarterly/semi-annual payments)
    • You're investing for 2+ years
    • You want the option to trade on NSE
    • You're building a diversified portfolio

    💡 The Balanced Approach

    Consider a mix: 60% T-bills for safety and liquidity, 40% corporate bonds for higher returns. This gives you the best of both worlds.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is the minimum amount to invest in T-bills?

    ▼

    The minimum investment for Treasury Bills is KES 50,000. For corporate bonds, it's typically KES 50,000 to KES 100,000.

    Are T-bills safer than corporate bonds?

    ▼

    Yes, T-bills are backed by the government and considered virtually risk-free. Corporate bonds carry issuer risk - the company might default.

    How are T-bill returns taxed?

    ▼

    Both T-bills and corporate bonds are subject to 15% withholding tax on interest, deducted at source.

    Can I sell my T-bills before maturity?

    ▼

    Yes, you can sell T-bills in the secondary market through CBK, but it's less liquid than corporate bonds traded on NSE.

    Which gives better returns currently?

    ▼

    Currently (2024), corporate bonds offer 12-16% while T-bills offer 10-14%. Corporate bonds typically yield 1-3% more than T-bills for the extra risk.

    How do I buy corporate bonds?

    ▼

    You need a CDS account with a stockbroker. Apply during new bond offers or buy existing bonds on the NSE through your broker.

    Conclusion

    Both Treasury Bills and Corporate Bonds have their place in a well-diversified investment portfolio. T-bills offer unmatched safety and short-term flexibility, while corporate bonds provide higher returns and regular income. The right choice depends on your risk tolerance, investment horizon, and income needs.
